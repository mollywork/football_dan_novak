Pushed too far: Overexertion has claimed lives of 22 Division I football players since 2000

By Dan Novak

Bennie “Buster’’ Abram III died his first day of practice as a defensive back for the University of Mississippi.

As winter workouts began Feb. 19, 2010, Abram told the coaching staff he felt under the weather and requested to not participate, according to documents from a wrongful death lawsuit. But coaches told Abram, a 20-year-old community college transfer student and walk-on, that if he didn’t join his teammates, he would be off the team.

After stretching and a half-mile run, players performed strength and agility drills at 10 different stations, which some Ole Miss players described as the hardest offseason workout. Abram collapsed after the first station and was castigated by coaches and pushed to continue, court documents say.

At the second station, he again collapsed and was helped to the sidelines. As players lined up for wind sprints, Abram was transported to the hospital and died later that day.

Abram was one of 22 Division I football players between 2000 and 2018 to die from an exertion-related illness suffered during a workout or practice, according to an analysis by the Howard Center for Investigative Journalism and The Shirley Povich Center for Sports Journalism at the University of Maryland. There were no reported deaths in 2019 or 2020. The causes of death include collapse from sickle cell trait, heatstroke and sudden cardiac arrest.

Across all of collegiate athletics, far more football players have died from these conditions than other athletes, according to data compiled by the National Center for Catastrophic Sport Injury Research at the University of North Carolina.

Exertion-related deaths result from intense exercise and are preventable with smarter, better-planned workouts, according to reform advocates.

Critics say the NCAA has not done enough to protect collegiate athletes from dangerous and sometimes fatal workouts. Coaches overseeing practices that result in the deaths of players are almost never fired from their positions, and even if they are, sometimes go on to new positions at different programs, according to an analysis by the Howard and Povich centers.

The NCAA strictly regulates recruiting violations and improper payments to athletes. From 2014 to 2018, the NCAA found 152 infractions of student-athletes receiving financial aid or payment, or other recruiting violations. The NCAA has not levied penalties on any of the 22 athletic programs that had a player die on its watch, but has made changes in response to the deaths of athletes.

Penn State running back Akeel Lynch (22) tries to turn the corner on Buffalo defensive end Solomon Jackson (41) during the first half of an NCAA college football game in State College, Pa, Saturday, Sept. 12, 2015. (AP Photo/Gene J. Puskar)

When players die, football programs often defend themselves by blaming the athletes or asserting whatever workout caused the illness was the same as any other -- as the University of Central Florida did following the death of Ereck Plancher and Northwestern University did after Rashidi Wheeler died.

“It’s preventable death,” said Devard Darling, former player at Florida State whose twin brother and teammate Devaughn died from a conditioning workout in 2001. “And it’s under the hands and in the care of these coaches and trainers who are supposed to be there to take care of us. You go into family’s homes, you sit down, you talk to their parents, you tell them how you’re going to take care of them and help them become men, and this and that. No one sends their kids off to school to die.”

Institutions have paid out tens of millions in settlements to victims’ families, including $3.5 million in January to the family of Jordan McNair, a University of Maryland player who died from heatstroke in 2018.

The NCAA has made incremental progress in protecting athletes over the past two decades. But even the NCAA acknowledges it needs to do more to hold individuals and institutions accountable.

NCAA Chief Medical Officer Brian Hainline recognized there is a “gap” when it comes to holding coaches and programs responsible.

“That’s sort of the next step ... how do we actually legislate health and safety?” Hainline said in an interview.

No Ole Miss trainer, coach or athletic department staff member was deemed to be responsible for Abram’s death, either by the university or the NCAA. Team personnel did not break any NCAA rules when conducting the training regimen that led to Abram’s death, and the university claimed athletics personnel followed NCAA best practices in caring for an athlete with sickle cell trait.

The Rebels’ head strength and conditioning coach, Don Decker, was subsequently named that year’s Collegiate Strength and Conditioning Coach of the Year by the Professional Football Strength and Conditioning Coaches Association.

Devaughn Darling with his twin brother Devard at the Florida State University football game. (Photo courtesy of As One Foundation)

“Unfortunately almost all the deaths come from just completely inappropriate strength and conditioning sessions. That’s the single biggest cause presenting all those conditions,” said Douglas Casa, CEO of the Korey Stringer Institute at the University of Connecticut, which educates youth, high school, collegiate and professional athletes on how to prevent illnesses and death that result from exertion. The institute is named after the former Minnesota Vikings offensive lineman who died from heatstroke in 2001.

“It’s such a travesty that we allow these big-time college programs to abuse these athletes,” said Casa, also a Ph.D. of exercise physiology.

A 2017 NCAA investigation resulted in Ole Miss forfeiting all of its wins spanning six seasons, including the year Abram died. The infraction? “An unconstrained culture of booster involvement in football recruiting.”

“This is an association … that enforces all kinds of frivolous rules, like preventing players from receiving a free meal from a third party, or a shelter, or entering into a name, image and likeness deal,” said Ramogi Huma, executive director of the National College Players Association, a nonprofit organization that advocates for student-athletes’ rights. Huma founded the organization while he was a football player for UCLA in the late 1990s.

“You have to question whether or not the colleges and the NCAA prioritizes players’ health,” he said. “And absolutely not. It doesn’t.”

Often, intense conditioning drills are grounded in a masculine football culture with the belief that hard workouts build toughness and team character.

University of Maryland head coach DJ Durkin told the Carroll County Times in 2016 that he tried to make summer practices as hard as possible. "The heat makes cowards out of us all,” he said. “We're working on some mental toughness, and that's what we're going to do.”

Two years later, when McNair, a Maryland offensive lineman, became ill while running sprints, head athletic trainer Wes Robinson reportedly yelled to “get him the (expletive) up” and “drag his ass across the field.”

In 2019, 49% of Division I football players were Black and are at far greater risk of carrying the trait than players of other races and ethnicities. Of the 22 players to die from exertion since 2000, all but two were Black.

Abram was not in poor physical fitness before starting at Ole Miss. He had been playing football for Itawamba Community College in rural Mississippi, and subsequently received several scholarship offers.

After making the Rebels, Abram, like every football player, underwent an extensive medical evaluation. A blood test revealed he carried the sickle cell trait, like roughly 8% of the African American population. But according to the lawsuit, neither he nor his family was ever informed.

According to the National Athletic Trainers’ Association, although carrying the gene is benign, intense and sustained physical stress can cause red blood cells of those with the trait to change shape and reduce blood flow — similar to the red blood cells of people who suffer from sickle cell anemia. This reduction in blood flow can lead to rapid breakdown of muscle tissue and cell death.

“I was able to work hard and do everything, it was the recovery part that got me,” Darling said, who like his twin brother, also carries the sickle cell trait but is able to manage his symptoms. “I’ve had a career, I’ve worked just as hard as anyone out there. It’s just knowing your body. Awareness and being educated is the biggest thing.”

Darling went on to play five years in the NFL and currently runs a foundation teaching young athletes and the public about the sickle cell trait.

An analysis by the Povich and Howard centers found that from 2000 to 2010, nine Division I football players with sickle cell trait died. In 2019, 49% of Division I football players were Black and are at far greater risk of carrying the trait than players of other races and ethnicities. Of the 22 players to die from exertion since 2000, all but two were Black.

The NCAA did not start mandating schools to test for sickle cell trait until August 2010 as a part of a wrongful death settlement on behalf of Rice University player Dale Lloyd II, who died in 2006. One player has died from a sickling collapse in the 10 years since testing began.

Dale Lloyd runs out onto the field with his teammates before the Rice University football game. (Photo courtesy of Rice University)

Abram’s family received $50,000 from the insurance company for Mississippi's athletic foundation and $275,000 from the NCAA’s insurance policy in a settlement. Since 2000, Division I institutions have paid more than $29.5 million to resolve 12 wrongful death and negligence lawsuits, either through settlements or court order.

Eleven families received money in damages on behalf of their sons who died from either sickle cell trait collapse or exertional heatstroke. One family settled a lawsuit after a player died from an exercise-induced asthma attack. Three lawsuits were settled for undisclosed amounts and are not included in the above figure.

The largest settlement was $16 million for the death of Rashidi Wheeler at Northwestern University, a private school. It can be harder to bring lawsuits against public universities, where sovereign immunity protects states from being tried in civil court and caps damages.

The family of Ereck Plancher, a player from the University of Central Florida who died in 2008, was awarded $10 million in a wrongful death lawsuit that was decided by a jury trial. However, the university athletic association appealed the case to the Florida Supreme Court, which limited the damages to $200,000 due to sovereign immunity.

“A couple hundred thousand dollars ain’t much of a deterrent to make you do anything when your program brings in $200 million,” said Eugene Egdorf, an attorney who represented the Abram and Lloyd families.

Just two deaths of a football player from indirect causes — Tyler Heintz at Kent State University in 2017 and McNair at the University of Maryland in 2018 — led to dismissals of team staff.

After Heintz died of heatstroke, it was discovered that strength and conditioning coach Ross Bowsher, who was supervising the drills that led to Heintz’s death, falsified his professional certification. Nonetheless, the university claimed, “the workout was conducted in accordance with national protocols for student-athlete health and safety.”

At Maryland, it was only after news reports and an internal investigation found McNair had not been properly treated for heatstroke and coaches created a culture of abuse on the team that the university fired four members of the training and coaching staff.

Durkin, football head strength and conditioning coach Rick Court, Robinson and assistant athletic director of athletic training Steve Nordwall were all dismissed, but only after details of McNair’s improper care went public following his death. Durkin was eventually reinstated by the University System of Maryland Board of Regents, but UMD President Wallace Loh fired him shortly thereafter. Durkin received at least $5 million in severance pay and found an assistant coaching job less than two years later at Ole Miss.

One coach oversaw conditioning sessions that led to two players’ deaths. Robert Jackson was the trainer at the University of Central Florida in 2008 when Plancher collapsed in an offseason workout from sickle cell trait and died. Jackson later became the head football trainer at the University of California, Berkeley, where another player, Ted Agu, died of sickling collapse in 2014.

A spokesman for California-Berkeley would not provide the Howard Center with any details surrounding Jackson’s hiring, but said the athletic department was “aware that Mr. Jackson was an assistant trainer at UCF” when Plancher died.

November 29, 2014: California defensive ends Todd Barr (41) and Brennan Scarlett (17) carry the jersey of teammate Ted Agu who died during off-season conditioning before the NCAA football game between the California Golden Bears and the BYU Cougars at Memorial Stadium in Berkeley, CA. (Icon Sportswire via AP Images)

As Plancher struggled during a conditioning session, UCF head coach George O’Leary ordered water and trainers to be removed from the practice facility, according to three players who testified in court. O’Leary and three other players disputed this in their testimony.

The Orlando Sentinel reported O’Leary swore at Plancher when he collapsed during the workout, which included a 75-minute weightlifting session, sprints and agility drills. “I remember having blurred vision when I was trying to do the offensive drills,” Cody Minnich, a UCF player who participated in the workout, testified in court. “The majority of it was just spent like being dizzy.”

UCF Athletic Director Keith Tribble, a University of Florida Athletic Hall of Fame inductee as a former offensive guard, called the workout “basic” during a press conference the day of Plancher’s death.

Plancher, like Abram, was not informed he had sickle cell trait, a lawsuit states. Nine months later, another UCF player collapsed at practice and was hospitalized with kidney failure. O’Leary stayed on to coach at UCF until 2015 and is now immortalized with a statue outside UCF’s Bounce House stadium.

In 2001, Wheeler, the Northwestern safety, collapsed while running a set of wind sprints during preseason conditioning tests. Coroners ruled his death was caused by exercise-induced asthma. Days after his death, Northwestern head coach Randy Walker said, “I have no doubt a better-conditioned athlete is a whole lot more likely to survive the game of football. I didn’t write the rules. But it is a rigorous sport.”

On a day in 2005 when media were invited to watch a voluntary practice at the University of Missouri, linebacker Aaron O'Neal showed signs of fatigue during agility exercises. He complained of blurry vision and dropped to his hands and knees, but coaches told him to repeat the drill three more times. After O’Neal fell unconscious, staff took him to the team offices. He was taken to the hospital and died shortly after he arrived — about 90 minutes after the workout ended, a delay that may have cost him his life.

“Killing a player in a hazardous workout is not against the rules,” Huma said. “Physically assaulting a player is not against NCAA rules.”

“It’s such a travesty that we allow these big-time college programs to abuse these athletes...If any of them had sickle cell trait, they very possibly could have died that day.”

― Douglas Casa, CEO of the Korey Stringer Institute at the University of Connecticut

Fatal accidents from football have significantly decreased since the 1960s and 1970s because of rule changes to make the game safer, protecting athletes from head and neck injuries. But deaths from overexertion started spiking when the sport “went from seasonal to year-round training” around 2000, said Scott Anderson, head athletic trainer at the University of Oklahoma and president of the College Athletic Trainers’ Society. According to his research about offseason training, six football players across all NCAA divisions died from traumatic injuries between 2000 and 2016.

According to an analysis by the Povich and Howard centers, eight football players died of exertion-related illnesses on the first day of practice following a break or offseason. Athletic training professionals say the first week of activity can be risky if coaches rush into overly strenuous workouts for players who may be out of football shape following an offseason. Workouts with no basis in sports science cause these illnesses, Anderson said.

“The onus is upon the strength coaches for those workouts,” he said. “They should be planned, there should be some sport-specificity there, there should be some exercise-science basis, and there should be consideration for individual risk factors.”

In May 2018, the 19-year-old McNair, a 325-pound lineman, suffered heatstroke while running 10 110-yard sprints. McNair was not treated promptly enough, his body was unable to recover and he died two weeks later.

Medical experts have found preventing heatstroke death is simple, provided training staff can identify the problem and immerse the player in an ice bath. The survival rate for an athlete with heat-related illness is 100% if their body temperature is brought below 104 degrees within 10 minutes of a medical event.

During football workouts, players are often grouped together regardless of size, ability or conditioning level, and all are expected to perform the same drills, according to Casa. McNair, a lineman who would never sprint longer than a few seconds on a single play over the course of an actual game, was running the same sprints as smaller players like receivers and backs, according to a University of Maryland investigation.

Most players who suffer from heat illness are big, heavy players. Although practicing in high heat and humidity is an added risk, Casa said, athletes will adjust to the weather over the first seven to 10 days of practice if workouts are safe and players are provided with enough time to rest and hydrate.

Anderson said McNair, Abram and other players should never be put in the position to suffer heatstroke or sickle cell collapse in the first place.

“Is there an exercise science that says 110 yards is the magic number?” Anderson said. “Is there an exercise science in terms of how fast it should be run? Is there an exercise science that says how many should be run? Is there an exercise science about what the rest and recovery should be? Is there an exercise science behind this? And the answer is no.”

Aaron O'Neal kneels in his football uniform to pose for a photo. (Photo courtesy University of Missouri)

Other serious muscular and skeletal conditions can result from dangerous conditioning sessions. At the University of Iowa in 2011,13 players were hospitalized with rhabdomyolysis after a weightlifting session that included 100 squats at 50% maximum weight. The workout demonstrated to the coaching staff “who wanted to be on the team,” according to a university investigation.

Frequently caused by extreme physical stress, rhabdomyolysis causes a severe breakdown of muscle tissue, which can result in kidney failure and death, according to the Cleveland Clinic. Other rhabdomyolysis outbreaks occurred at the University of Oregon in 2017 and the University of Nebraska in 2018.

“If any of them had sickle cell trait, they very possibly could have died that day,” Casa said.

Iowa strength and conditioning coach Chris Doyle was named a master strength and conditioning coach by the Collegiate Strength and Conditioning Coaches Association in 2012. He eventually became the highest-paid strength coach in the country before leaving Iowa in June 2020 after multiple allegations of racial bias among players.

When Agu, the defensive lineman for California-Berkeley, collapsed from repeated sprints up and down a hill while holding a rope, his death was originally attributed to a sudden cardiac arrest from an abnormally thick heart. After reading players' accounts with more detailed circumstances of his collapse, the medical examiner changed the cause of death to sickle cell trait.

It can be difficult to differentiate the cause of death among heatstroke, sickle cell collapse and cardiac death, although autopsies have improved in recent years, according to Kim Harmon, head football physician at the University of Washington and a leading expert on cardiac issues in athletes. Leading critics like Anderson and Casa are adamant that many exertional sickle cell deaths are wrongly attributed to heart problems.

The Howard and Povich centers found that seven players’ deaths since 2000 were ruled as sudden cardiac failures, the leading cause of death during exercise for all college athletes. According to Harmon’s research, sudden cardiac death among athletes is primarily brought about by underlying heart conditions. But Anderson argues that although exertion is not the sole cause of cardiac arrest, almost all sudden cardiac deaths among college football players occur during practice and conditioning sessions, proving exertion is a trigger.

“It’s almost a sigh of relief [when an autopsy is ruled a cardiac failure],” Anderson said. “That’s God’s fault, we can’t be held accountable for that. But we can potentially be held accountable for exertional heatstroke, we can be held accountable potentially for exertional sickling, we can be held accountable potentially for asthma death. But cardiac, who could have known? Who could have done anything about that?”

Just one lawsuit has been brought stemming from a player dying of sudden cardiac arrest, and it was dismissed.

Claude Terell, step-father of Keeley Dorsey, and Tammie Dorsey, mother of Keeley Dorsey, spoke at a news conference about the death of their son, Tuesday, Jan. 23, 2007, in Tallahassee, Fla. Dorsey, a football player for South Florida, died after collapsing during a team conditioning workout at the school's athletic facility. (AP Photo/Phil Coale)

Along with the rule mandating that players be tested for sickle cell trait, the NCAA has taken some steps to mitigate exertional deaths, with mixed success.

In 2003, the NCAA passed several rules governing preseason practice time, including restrictions on two-a-day practices (which the NCAA has since banned) and limits on the number of daily practice hours. The NCAA also started requiring a mandatory five-day acclimatization period for football players to build fitness as preseason practice begins. Before 2003, one to two heatstroke deaths occurred during August practices each year, according to research in the Journal of Athletic Training.

since then, one player died during the first week of preseason practice, one died during his first practice while trying out for the team and six other players died during the first week of winter, spring or voluntary workouts, none of which are covered by the NCAA legislation.

Also in 2003, the NCAA gave athletics training staff the “unchallengeable authority” to provide independent medical care and end unsafe workouts during voluntary offseason workouts. Yet, according to a survey conducted in 2019 by the National Athletic Trainers’ Association, nearly a quarter of college trainers still feel they do not have that authority.

“If my existence here at the University of Oklahoma centers on keeping (head football coach) Lincoln Riley happy … at some point in time I’m not going to be making best interest medical decisions for our athletes,” Anderson said. “If the athletic director is ceding that authority to the coach over the medical providers, or the athletic director and athletic administration isn’t supporting the medical staff as they make those decisions, then we don’t have independent care.”

In 2015, the NCAA began mandating strength and conditioning coaches from Division I programs attain professional accredited certification. But as a 2017 CBS Sports investigation found, strength coaches can gain their credentials in some questionable certification programs, including one that lasts just 21 hours. Court, the Maryland strength and conditioning coach who led the workout that resulted in McNair’s heatstroke, earned the title of “master” strength and conditioning coach in 2016. The CSCCa calls it “undoubtedly the highest honor” bestowed on strength and conditioning coaches.

The college football season continued in 2020 despite the COVID-19 pandemic. COVID-19 outbreaks around the country cancelled in-person classes and sent students home, but with billions of dollars on the line, all of the Power 5 Conferences played football games.

The risk from sudden cardiac death has taken another turn during the pandemic, as myocarditis — inflammation of the heart — has emerged as a common condition in recovered COVID-19 patients. According to an Ohio State University study of 26 college athletes who had tested positive for the coronavirus, 15% showed signs of developing the condition. A 2015 study found that of 64 college athletes who died from cardiac arrest with a specific cause determined, 9% suffered from myocarditis.

In 2016, a task force convened by the NCAA released a set of guidelines on cardiovascular care of student-athletes, including a recommendation to screen all athletes before they participate. Harmon, a coauthor of the document, said heart electrocardiograms, if properly performed and interpreted, can detect two-thirds to three-quarters of abnormalities that lead to death. Even the most exhaustive preventive measures will not detect every heart condition that could lead to death, she said.

“[The NCCA doesn't] want to pass any rules because they feel like if they pass a rule and something doesn’t get done right, somebody like me is going to go sue them. And they’d rather have no rules and live with kids getting sick or die and risk getting sued.”

― Eugene Egdorf, an attorney who represented the Abram and Lloyd families

Not until 2019, a year after McNair died, did the NCAA release comprehensive guidelines on preventing sudden death in athletes from ailments such as brain injuries, spine injuries and asthma, as well as sudden cardiac arrest, exertional sickling collapse and exertional heatstroke. The document gives athletic programs a list of guidelines to follow, including refraining from using exercise punitively, making sure workouts are based in science and are sport-specific, and ensuring staff have emergency action plans to treat athletes with serious illnesses.

Hainline, the NCAA’s chief medical officer, said the “most important” aspect of the 2019 best-practices document is a recommendation that strength and conditioning coaches no longer report to the head coaching staff but report to sports medicine personnel, ensuring independent medical care, reflecting a more important role for athletic trainers and physicians. The document also recommends institutions discipline coaches and staff that do not follow the guidelines.

“This is not my belief today, but for a very long time (the NCAA) did not prioritize health and safety,” Casa said. “I think the NCAA has done more in the past three or four years than they did in the last hundred years prior.”

The NCAA still does not have the power to hold programs or coaches accountable for pushing athletes past their limit. It is not against NCAA rules to do so.

“They don’t want to pass any rules because they feel like if they pass a rule and something doesn’t get done right, somebody like me is going to go sue them,” Egdorf said. “And they’d rather have no rules and live with kids getting sick or die and risk getting sued.”

Ole Miss lawyers defended the conditioning workout that led to Abram’s death by pointing out that any NCAA recommendation is not a “statute, ordinance, or regulation.”

Player advocates like Egdorf and Huma will be unmoved by NCAA efforts until coaches and institutions face consequences.

“This is not a matter of it being difficult to identify appropriate health and safety standards,” Huma said. “The problem is that these guidelines are not enforced. They’re not adopted as actual rules that are enforced, and no one's held accountable when they break these best practices.”

A note on methodology:
This story focuses on 22 NCAA Division I football players who died from an exertion-related illness suffered during a practice or conditioning session from 2000-2018. The primary causes of death for these incidents are sudden cardiac arrest, exertional sickling collapse and exertional heatstroke. One player died of an exercise-induced asthma attack. The story does not include players who died from sudden cardiac arrest unrelated to football practice or athletes who died from medical issues or accidents not tied to exertion. The research was based on cases gathered from publicly available court records, legislative reports, media reports and interviews.


22 lives claimed: A visual timeline from 2000 to 2018



Preston Birdsong (Photo courtesy of Tennessee Tech University)

Preston Birdsong, Tennessee Tech University

Age: 18
Date of death: Aug. 13, 2000

The 5-foot-11, 190-pound Birdsong was reportedly in good shape when he collapsed while running sprints on the first day of preseason workouts. The freshman defensive back’s death was originally attributed to heatstroke, but was later connected to sickle cell trait. University spokeswoman Monica Greppin said at the time that the practice “was a pretty light workout.”

Devaughn Darling, Florida State University

Age: 18
Date of death: Feb. 26, 2001

The 6-foot-2, 220-pound Darling collapsed after a 75-minute continuous conditioning workout known as “mat drills,” designed to push players to their limits over 10 consecutive days during offseason training. The sophomore linebacker complained to his teammates but not to coaches of pain in his chest and depleted vision. Coaches made him finish the drills. After collapsing, Darling was rushed to the hospital, where he died. An autopsy revealed Darling carried the sickle cell trait. His parents filed a lawsuit against Florida State alleging coaches and trainers were responsible for the death, which was settled for $2 million. Florida’s sovereign immunity law caps settlements from state institutions at $200,000 unless the state legislature approves a higher amount via a claims bill, so the family did not see the remaining $1.8 million until state legislation passed in 2017. Darling’s twin brother and teammate Devard, who also tested positive for the trait, claimed he was kicked off the team for being a liability. He transferred to play at Washington State University. After Darling’s death, Florida State continued the mat drills with far more precautions, including more rest time, water coolers and an ambulance on standby.

Devaughn Darling (Photo courtesy of As One Foundation)

Eraste Autin (Photo courtesy of University of Florida)

Eraste Autin, University of Florida

Age: 18
Date of death: Jul. 25, 2001

Autin was participating in a voluntary summer workout on July 19, 2001, which included 50 minutes of stretching, sprints and agility drills. The freshman fullback looked disoriented while returning to the locker room, so a bystander got him to stop and lie down outside the stadium. He was taken to the hospital and six days later, Autin died from complications related to heatstroke. Autin’s family filed a wrongful death lawsuit against the University of Florida Athletic Association and received an undisclosed settlement.

Rashidi Wheeler, Northwestern University

Age: 22
Date of death: Aug. 3, 2001

The senior safety was going through a preseason conditioning test when he struggled to breathe during a set of wind sprints. At least three other players collapsed during the drill. Coaches and paramedics performed CPR on Wheeler, who was athsmatic, before he had to be taken to the hospital, where he died later that day. The medical examiner ruled the death from exercise-induced bronchial asthma.

In the days after Wheeler’s death, head coach Randy Walker said, “I have no doubt a better-conditioned athlete is a whole lot more likely to survive the game of football. I didn’t write the rules. But it is a rigorous sport.” Wheeler’s mother filed a wrongful death lawsuit against Northwestern, which was settled for $16 million.

Rashidi Wheeler (Photo courtesy of Northwestern University)

Jawan Jackson, Northern Illinois University

Age: 19
Date of death: Feb. 1, 2002

Jackson, a freshman attempting to walk on the team, collapsed on his second day of conditioning drills. Jackson showed no sign of struggle through most of the workout, until about halfway through wind sprints. Results of an autopsy were inconclusive, but noted that Jackson had an enlarged heart. Jackson was a football and track team captain in high school.

May 2003: NCAA implements several rules governing preseason practice time, including restrictions on two-a-day practices and limits on the number of daily practice hours. The NCAA also mandates a mandatory five-day “acclimatization” period to allow players to ease into workouts as football practice resumes after the offseason.

May 2003: NCAA implements bylaw empowering sports medicine staff “with the unchallengeable authority to cancel or modify the workout for health and safety reasons” during voluntary conditioning activities conducted by a strength coach.

Chris Waddell (Photo courtesy of Northwestern State University)

Chris Waddell, Northwestern State University

Age: 19
Date of death: Mar. 1, 2004

Waddell joined the Northwestern State team as a walk-on and was participating in his first spring conditioning workout with the team when he suddenly collapsed. Athletic trainers tended to Waddell, attempting to resuscitate him, but he was pronounced dead at the hospital. The coroner specified the official cause of death as a heart attack, related to a heart condition that Waddell was diagnosed with when he was a child. His mother said that her son had received treatment for the condition and was medically cleared for all activities. Waddell passed a medical exam that past August and was permitted to play.

McCollins Umeh, University of Arizona

Age: 18
Date of death: Jun. 8, 2004

Umeh reported for the first voluntary workout with Arizona. That morning, he underwent a physical exam before heading out into 95-degree heat. He collapsed 15 minutes into the workout and was taken to a nearby medical center. Umeh was pronounced dead less than an hour later. The coroner found in the autopsy that he had an enlarged heart. Umeh was the second Arizona football player in a decade to die after a workout — Damon Terrell died in 1995 after collapsing during practice.

Aaron Richardson, Bowling Green State University

Age: 18
Date of death: Sep. 15, 2004

A three-sport high school athlete trying to walk on to BGSU’s football team, the freshman Richardson died after his first practice. The practice started with a series of sprints for about 10 minutes — known as “gassers.” Richardson complained of calf cramps afterward and was allegedly cursed at by Defensive Coordinator Tim Beckman for failing to continue. He was taken to the locker room where he became unresponsive, and the assistant athletic trainer called 911. Less than two hours later, he was pronounced dead at the hospital.

An autopsy revealed Richardson died from complications surrounding sickle cell trait. Richardson’s mother filed a wrongful death suit against the university, but the Court of Claims of Ohio and an appeals court found the university not liable. Richardson was the second BGSU athlete to die within two years while playing for the school — soccer player Leslie Dawley collapsed on the field during a game in 2002 and later died.

Aaron O’Neal (Photo courtesy of University of Missouri)

Aaron O’Neal, University of Missouri

Age: 19
Date of death: Jul. 12, 2005

St. Louis native O’Neal was a 19-year-old redshirt freshman heading into the 2005 season with Missouri. He started to struggle during agility drills about 45 minutes into a preseason voluntary workout, which the media was invited to observe. O’Neal told coaches, “I’m trying. I’m not weak. I just can’t go on,” court records say. A teammate was chastised for helping him back to the locker room. Rather than taking O’Neal immediately to a nearby hospital when his condition worsened, coaches sent him in a campus truck to the team offices. A trainer called 911, and O’Neal died soon after he arrived at the hospital — about 90 minutes after the workout ended. The medical examiner originally attributed the death to viral meningitis, but during litigation, experts called the cause of death into question and attributed it to sickle cell trait.

The O’Neal family won $2 million in the wrongful death settlement against the university, in addition to $250,000 to a scholarship fund at the University of Missouri in Aaron’s name.

Dale Lloyd II, Rice University

Age: 19
Date of death: Sep. 25, 2006

Rice players who saw a lot of playing time in the previous day’s game reported for practice the following day to undergo a vigorous weight training and conditioning workout. After lifting weights, the players needed to run 16 100-yard sprints, each at 18 seconds or less. Shortly after beginning the sprints, Lloyd became short of breath, and his muscles started tightening. Coaches did nothing to help Lloyd when it became clear he was suffering from physical distress, court documents say. Some players tried to help their teammate, but a coach told them to leave Lloyd alone. He collapsed on the field and lost consciousness. He died the following day from collapse due to sickle cell trait.

Lloyd’s family received an undisclosed settlement in a wrongful death suit against the university. As a part of a settlement, the NCAA started advising its members to test players for sickle cell trait, which led to a testing requirement.

Keeley Dorsey, University of South Florida

Age: 19
Date of death: Jan. 17, 2007

Dorsey passed out during an offseason conditioning workout, and he was given immediate first-aid attention. Dorsey was later transported to the university hospital, where he died. A medical examiner determined that the 5-foot-11, 210-pound freshman running back may have suffered from an existing heart condition that led to his fatal collapse, but DNA tests were unable to confirm it. The family hired a law firm to pursue possible litigation, but opted not to file suit against the university.

Ereck Plancher, University of Central Florida

Age: 19
Date of death: Mar. 18, 2008

During an offseason workout, Plancher was participating in mat drills, which consisted of weight training, conditioning, agility drills, sprints and gassers that “push many of the UCF football team’s members beyond the point of exhaustion,” court documents say. During the workout, Plancher started experiencing dizziness, shortness of breath and an inability to verbally respond to questions, according to his family’s lawsuit.

Three players testified that head coach George O’Leary ordered water and trainers to be removed from the field, though O’Leary and three other players disputed this in testimony. According to the Orlando Sentinel, O’Leary swore at Plancher when he fell during the workout. “I remember having blurred vision when I was trying to do the offensive drills,” Cody Minnich, a former UCF player, testified in court. “The majority of it was just spent like being dizzy.”

Plancher died from complications due to sickle cell trait.

A jury ruled the UCF Athletics Association negligent in Plancher’s death and ordered the family be given $10 million. However, the Florida Supreme Court ruled on appeal that damages must be limited to $200,000 because of state sovereign immunity laws.

Ereck Plancher (Photo courtesy of University of Central Florida)

Chad Edward Wiley, North Carolina A&T State University

Age: 22
Date of death: May 28, 2008

The two-year starting offensive lineman collapsed after a voluntary offseason workout that began with 45 minutes of weightlifting, and proceeded with sprints up and down a hill. He complained of dizziness when returning to the locker room and lost consciousness shortly thereafter. He died the following day. His death was attributed to heatstroke, but the medical examiner ruled that sickle cell trait played a role in his death. His mother sued the university and received a $350,000 settlement, according to the North Carolina Department of Justice.

In 2010, Jospin Milandu died from collapse from sickle cell trait while trying out for North Carolina A&T’s track team. Athletic trainer Roland Lovelace was fired after emails emerged revealing he advised coaches against testing for the condition.

Ja’Quayvin Smalls, Western Carolina University

Age: 20
Date of death: Jul. 8, 2009

Junior defensive back Ja’Quayvin Smalls, a transfer student, died following his first voluntary offseason practice with the team. About 90 minutes into the workout, Smalls complained of cramps. While he was being treated, Smalls stopped breathing. He was transported to an area hospital where he was pronounced dead.

Court documents say Smalls’ mother disclosed to the university in a questionnaire that he carried the sickle cell trait, according to The Post and Courier (Charleston, South Carolina). The Smalls family settled with Western Carolina for $600,000, but the agreement does not include any admission of guilt by the athletics program. After Smalls’ death, the university began mandatory sickle cell testing.

August 2010: NCAA begins sickle cell solubility testing of student-athletes who are beginning their first season or are trying out for a team. Athletes may opt out of the test if they desire.

August 2012: The National Athletic Trainers’ Association releases “The Inter-Association Task Force for Preventing Sudden Death in Collegiate Conditioning Sessions: Best Practices Recommendations,” endorsed by 11 medical, training and sports science organizations. The NCAA does not endorse the document.

Bennie “Buster” Abram III (Photo courtesy of University of Mississippi)

Bennie “Buster” Abram III, University of Mississippi

Age: 20
Date of death: Feb. 19, 2010

As the first day of winter workouts began, Abram told the coaching staff he felt under the weather and requested to not participate, according to documents from a wrongful death lawsuit. But coaches told the 20-year-old community college transfer student and walk-on that if he didn’t join his teammates he would be off the team.

After stretching and a half-mile run, players went through strength and agility drills at 10 different stations. Abram collapsed after the first drill, and was castigated by coaches and pushed to continue, court documents say. At the second station, Abram again collapsed, and was helped to the sidelines. As players lined up for wind sprints, Abram was transported to the hospital. He died about six hours after arriving at the hospital from complications connected to sickle cell trait.

Abram’s family received a $50,000 settlement from the university, and $275,000 from an NCAA insurance policy.

Wayne Jones III, Tennessee State University

Age: 19
Date of death: Nov. 7, 2012

A redshirt freshman defensive back, Jones suffered a cardiac arrest shortly after beginning a noncontact practice. A wrongful death lawsuit, which was eventually dismissed, claimed Jones said he did not feel well earlier in the day, and athletic personnel failed to provide Jones with adequate care after he collapsed during a “deep ball” drill for defensive backs. "No assistance was provided to him, and an unreasonable period of time elapsed before anyone approached him or attempted to provide any help," the lawsuit says. Tennessee courts dismissed the suit, ruling that TSU’s medical care “did not cause Mr. Jones’ death.”

Ted Agu, University of California, Berkeley

Age: 21
Date of death: Feb. 7, 2014

Agu, a defensive lineman, collapsed after repeated sprints up and down a hill while holding onto a rope. Head strength and conditioning coach Damon Harrington designed the new drill to try “something new, exciting, fresh, kind of keep the guys engaged,” he said in his deposition.

A lawsuit alleged that coaches ignored Agu as he was clearly struggling and falling before collapsing. The medical examiner originally attributed his death to cardiac arrest from hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, but changed the determination to collapse from sickle cell trait after learning the account of the players.

Agu was diagnosed with sickle cell trait in 2010, and the team’s head physician, Casey Batten, had informed the team that he should be handled with care. After testimony in a wrongful death lawsuit, it came to light that Batten and other team officials did not disclose to county coroners that Agu carried the trait. Head football trainer Robert Jackson observed the workout as Agu collapsed. He was previously the trainer who led the workout at the University of Central Florida when Ereck Plancher died of sickle cell collapse.

California-Berkeley admitted liability after the suit and settled with the family for $4.75 million.

August 2014: NCAA starts requiring member schools to submit data on student-athlete fatalities, near-fatalities and catastrophic injuries.

California defensive ends Todd Barr (41) and Brennan Scarlett (17) carry the jersey of teammate Ted Agu who died during off-season conditioning before the NCAA football game between the California Golden Bears and the BYU Cougars at Memorial Stadium in Berkeley, CA. (Icon Sportswire via AP Images)

Marquese Meadow, Morgan State University

Age: 18
Date of Death: Aug. 24, 2014

Meadow was 18 when he collapsed from heatstroke during a preseason practice. The 6-foot-2, 300-pound freshman spent two weeks in the hospital before he died. A lawsuit claimed Meadow died during a “punishment practice,” where players ran for over an hour straight in the summer heat. A wrongful death lawsuit alleged that after Meadow was removed from the field, staff did not record his temperature accurately and tried to lower his body temperature by putting cold water on his groin and armpits.

The lawsuit ended with an undisclosed settlement. One year after Meadow’s death, head athletic trainer Michelle Daniels told The Baltimore Sun the team had not changed how it monitors or hydrates players during workouts.

August 2015: NCAA requires that strength coaches be certified through a nationally accredited certification program.

Solomon Jackson (Photo courtesy of University at Buffalo)

Solomon Jackson, University at Buffalo

Age: 20
Date of death: Feb. 29, 2016

Jackson, a sophomore redshirt defensive end, collapsed during the first offseason conditioning workout of the season. According to the University at Buffalo’s student newspaper, The Spectrum, it was a “nonstop” workout with hurdles, running and agility training. After collapsing, Jackson was sent to the hospital, where he died a week later. The university said in a statement that Jackson died of a “medical emergency,” and did not release any information on what led to his death, or his cause of death, citing privacy concerns. Autopsy reports were never released, but Jackson’s father said he died of “natural causes,” in an interview with The Spectrum.

April 2016: NCAA releases the “Interassociation Consensus Statement on Cardiovascular Care of College Student-Athletes,” guidelines for proper care for athletes who suffer cardiac arrest, and preventive measures.

Tyler Heintz, Kent State University

Age: 19
Date of death: Jun. 13, 2017

Heintz, a freshman, died from exertional heatstroke following conditioning drills during a preseason workout. It was the 6-foot-4, 275-pound offensive lineman’s second day of practice.

The university fired strength and conditioning coach Ross Bowsher, who oversaw the workout that led to Heintz’s death, after it was discovered he did not have the professional certification required. A university internal investigation found that the workout was conducted in “accordance with national protocols for student-athlete health and safety.”

Darius Minor, University of Maine

Age: 18
Date of death: Jul. 24, 2018

Fifteen minutes into an offseason workout, Minor told the strength and conditioning coach he felt like he was going to pass out. He collapsed soon after, and efforts to resuscitate him by team staff and emergency responders were unsuccessful.

School officials described the practice as a “supervised light workout,” which included sled drills with short breaks in between. The medical examiner ruled his death a sudden cardiac arrest. He had no known preexisting medical conditions and passed two physicals before starting practice.

Jordan McNair, University of Maryland

Age: 19
Date of death: Jun. 13, 2018

During the first late-spring conditioning workout May 29, McNair showed signs of exhaustion as he tried to finish 10, 110-yard sprints. After the seventh repetition, the 6-foot-4, 325-pound offensive lineman complained of dizziness and cramping. He finished the final three sprints. Head athletic trainer Wes Robinson, reportedly yelled to “drag his ass across the field.”

According to an internal investigation, staff tried to cool McNair’s body temperature by covering him with cold, wet towels. They did not immerse him in a cold-water bath, the surefire way to prevent heatstroke death. It took more than an hour and a half after McNair first started showing symptoms before he arrived at the hospital. By the time he reached the hospital, his body temperature had reached 106 degrees. He died two weeks later.

After ESPN released a report in August 2018 detailing a culture of abuse on the team, the university suspended head coach DJ Durkin and head strength and conditioning coach Rick Court. University President Wallace Loh then went on to say the school accepted "legal and moral responsibility for the mistakes" that led to McNair’s death.

After the internal investigation was released that October, the University System of Maryland Board of Regents determined there was not a toxic team culture and reinstated Durkin. However, Loh fired Durkin the following day. Durkin received more than $5 million in severance pay and Court received $315,000. Durkin has since been hired as an assistant coach at Ole Miss.

In January 2021, McNair’s family reached a settlement with the university for $3.5 million.

July 2019: NCAA releases “Preventing Catastrophic Injury and Death In Collegiate Athletics,” guidelines for institutions on preventing fatal illnesses such as heatstroke, sickle cell trait and sudden cardiac arrest. Twenty-two Division I football players died from exertion-related illnesses suffered during practices in the 18 years prior to the release of this document.
